Reclaiming Your Own Knowing

A Between-Session Homework

You have been doing harder work than anyone around you may know. The work of leaving a system that rearranged your inner life — a partner, a parent, a sibling, a caregiver, a family, a faith community, a teacher, a high-control group, a belief structure — is not a single event. It is a long, quiet undoing, mostly unwitnessed, and most of it happens inside you.

Coercive control works by teaching you to distrust your own intuition, to look outside yourself for what is true, to doubt the parts of you that said this isn't right. The signals that got called problems — your hesitation, your no, your discomfort, your questions — were actually the healthy parts of you trying to speak. They were right. The system needed you not to hear them.

Evan Stark calls this a liberty crime — one that does not merely inflict harm, but steals your freedom to be yourself. Smart, capable people stay inside coercive systems — partners, parents, siblings, caregivers, families, faith communities, groups — because these systems exploit the attachment mechanism, not because anyone is weak. What felt like loyalty, or love, or devotion was often a survival mechanism doing its job. Naming it that way is not an indictment of your heart. It's a way of giving your heart back its accurate story.

This handout is a slow practice of relistening. Not fixing. Not catching up. Just gently turning your attention back toward the knowing that was always there, and letting it have your ear again. Move through it at whatever pace feels right. Skip what doesn't land. Return to what does.

Part 1: Your Body Knows First

Your body registered what was happening before your mind could name it. Tight chest. Held breath. A stomach that would not settle. A small, persistent sense of something is wrong here that you learned to talk yourself out of. Your body was not betraying you. It was the first honest narrator you had.

This part is not about interpreting or fixing the sensations. It's just about letting the body speak again and being willing to listen without overriding it.

You might notice, without needing to solve anything:

• What does your chest feel like right now, when you think about the person or the system you're processing?

• What does it feel like to imagine making a small choice today that's just for you — no permission asked?

• Is there a place in your body that has gone quiet, or numb, or unreachable since you left? What might it be protecting?

• When you remember a specific moment you knew something was off — even if you couldn't say so — where in your body does that memory live?

• What is one sensation you're noticing right now that you would normally talk yourself out of?

Information, not judgment

A tight chest is information. A flutter in the stomach is information. A warmth in the shoulders is information. The body gives you data — it doesn't tell you what the data means. Meaning-making comes later, slowly, and usually in conversation with someone who can help you hold it. For now, just let the signals be signals. You don't have to decide anything about them today.

Part 2: The Shape of What Happened to You

Coercive control isn't one tactic. It's a system. Dr. Steven Hassan, who has spent decades studying high-control groups and relationships, named four dimensions most of these systems operate through — a framework he calls the BITE model: behavior, information, thought, and emotion. Not every system uses every lever with equal force, but most systems use all four.

Seeing the shape of the thing — even just having the words for it — can interrupt the private story that said this was about me, my weakness, my fault. It wasn't. It was a structure, and you were living inside it.

You don't need to catalogue every instance. This is an orientation, not an inventory. Read through and notice what your body does.

The BITE model, at a glance

  • Behavior — what you did, who you saw, how you spent time and money
  • Information — what you were allowed to know or hear
  • Thought — the categories, questions, and internal moves you were given
  • Emotion — which feelings were welcome, which weren't, and the fears that got installed

How behavior got shaped

What you wore. Who you saw. Where you went. How you spent money. How much sleep you got. Which of your friendships were allowed to continue and which quietly disappeared. Whether you had to ask permission for ordinary choices. How rewards and consequences arrived — warm one moment, icy the next — to reshape what you did.

You might notice:

• Is there a place in your daily life now where you still ask for permission in your head, even though no one is there to grant or deny it?

• What was the smallest regulated behavior — one that would look trivial from outside — that took the most from you?

How the information you had access to got shaped

What was kept from you. What was distorted. Whose perspectives you were allowed to hear, and whose were labeled dangerous, toxic, or unwell. How your history got rewritten in real time. How you came to see the outside world through their frame, because theirs was the only frame you were given.

This is the dimension that often produces the worst self-blame after leaving — how did I not see it? You didn't see it because you were inside a system designed to prevent you from seeing it. That's not a failure of your intelligence. That's the system working exactly as it was built to work.

You might notice:

• Are there parts of your own story, or the relationship's story, that you suspect you still don't have clear access to?

• Who did you lose contact with — quietly, gradually — that you might want to hear from again now, if only to hear a different version of reality?

How thinking got shaped

The categories you were given — good and bad, safe and dangerous, us and them. The questions that were off-limits. The thought-stopping moves that arrived automatically when doubt began to rise: a prayer, a phrase, a correction, a quick pivot away. A map of reality where only certain conclusions were even possible, so that disagreement felt like insanity rather than information.

You might notice:

• What is a question you weren't allowed to ask inside the system that you'd want to ask now?

• When a doubt begins to rise in you today, what's the first internal move you make? Whose voice is that move in?

How emotions got shaped

Which feelings were permitted, and which were treated as evidence of something wrong with you. The sudden swings between warmth and withdrawal that kept your nervous system oriented toward them. The guilt that showed up faster than thought. And the installed fears — about leaving, about questioning, about consequences — so precise and so catastrophic they could stop you from moving before you'd finished the thought.

Hassan calls this last one phobia indoctrination: not ordinary fear, but carefully engineered fear about what will happen if you step outside the system. These fears can outlast the system by years. They are often the last thing to leave. Noticing a catastrophic thought and gently asking whose fear is this? is slow work, but it's work that belongs to you now.

You might notice:

• Which feelings were welcome inside the system, and which weren't?

• Is there a specific fear about having left — about consequences, about being wrong, about being punished by something — that arrives uninvited? When you look at it directly, does it feel shaped like the voice of the system, or like a voice of your own?

Part 3: The Loaded Words

Every high-control system has its own language. Steven Hassan calls it loaded language — ordinary words that have been given a second, private meaning inside the system, so that hearing them activates an automatic response before your thinking mind catches up. A word like obedience may sound neutral to an outsider, but to you it may still land as a small shock of shame, fear, or self-correction.

Reclaiming your knowing includes reclaiming your language. Not rejecting every word the system used, but getting to choose again what a word means in the life you're authoring now.

You might notice:

• Name up to three words or phrases from the system that still activate shame, fear, or self-doubt when you encounter them.

• For each one — what did that word mean inside the system? Who decided its meaning?

• What might the same word mean to a neutral speaker — a friend, a stranger, someone who has never been inside what you've been inside?

• Is there a version of that word you'd be willing to keep, on your own terms? Or is it a word you'd rather set down for a while?

Words to try on, gently

A few examples of words that often carry loaded weight in high-control contexts — and how their meaning might be reclaimed in a life you actually author:

  • Obedience — inside the system: compliance that kept you safe from punishment. On your own terms: listening to what your own life is asking of you.
  • Submission — inside: surrender of will to an external authority. On your own terms: softening into what's true for you, even when it's hard.
  • Humility — inside: smallness, self-erasure. On your own terms: a clear-eyed sense of your own size, no more and no less.
  • Service — inside: proving your worth through labor. On your own terms: generosity that flows from a full cup, not an empty one.
  • Sacrifice — inside: giving what you couldn't afford to give. On your own terms: a choice you make with your eyes open, not one that's extracted from you.

Part 4: Authentic vs. Conditioned

In coercive systems, conditioned responses feel like moral truth. The reaction arrives fast — certain, absolute, and laced with a fear of consequences that may not exist anymore. After leaving, one of the most disorienting parts of healing is not knowing which of your reactions are yours and which were installed. It can feel like you don't know yourself at all. That's not a failure. It's what happens when a system is removed and the scaffolding it built inside you is still there.

If there's a period where you feel you don't know who you are anymore — not in the poetic sense, but in a genuinely unmooring way, a kind of catastrophic collapse of the self you were inside the system — that is not a failure. It's a precondition. The identity you built in there is being released, and the authentic self is returning, late and real. The void between the two is expected, protective, and temporary. Some people call it a dark night of the self. It is a passage, not a verdict.

You don't have to sort all of this out at once. You can just start to notice, without self-blame, when a should shows up, and gently ask: whose voice is this?

If the system you're leaving framed its control as God's will, spiritual growth, or the path to salvation, this work may touch conscience and moral identity, not just preferences — questioning can feel less like self-discovery and more like sin. That makes the sorting slower, and more tender. Go at the pace your soul can hold.

You might notice:

• Think of a recent moment a strong should showed up — about your body, your choices, your day, your relationships. Whose voice does it sound like?

• When you imagine not doing that should, what happens in your body? Does it tighten, or does something in you quietly open?

• Is there a preference, a taste, a small want you're aware of right now that would have been discouraged or punished inside the system? What does it feel like to let yourself notice it?

• Where in your life do you already know, clearly, what is yours? (It might be small. It counts.)

Three gentle tells

Over time, you may start to notice patterns that help you distinguish a conditioned response from an authentic one. None of these are rules — just soft orientation:

  • The body — conditioned responses often make the body tighten, brace, or shrink. Authentic responses more often come with a sense of openness or settling, even when the choice is hard.
  • The speed — conditioned responses tend to arrive fast, certain, and already-decided. Authentic responses are usually more considered, with room for I'm not sure yet.
  • The stakes — disobeying a conditioned rule can feel catastrophic, even when nothing bad will actually happen. Moving against a personal value feels uncomfortable, but not apocalyptic.

Part 5: Small Acts of Sovereignty

Autonomy does not return in one grand gesture. It returns in a thousand small choices you make, purely for yourself, in the ordinary moments of a day. The drink you actually want. The no you say without a paragraph of justification. The ten minutes you linger somewhere with no productive reason.

These choices may feel almost embarrassingly small — and that is exactly why they matter. The system was built out of small coercions. It gets dismantled in the same scale.

For some readers, this work is explicitly financial — opening your own account, making a purchase without asking, checking a balance you weren't allowed to see, filing a tax return in your own name, leaving a communal money structure, recovering from a family or group that held your earnings, your inheritance, or your credit. Financial coercion takes many shapes — a partner controlling access, a parent or sibling leveraging money as obedience, a caregiver or guardian controlling your resources, a group requiring tithes or communal finances, an employer or faith leader extracting labor. Each of these leaves its own kind of scaffolding inside. Rebuilding is not about spreadsheets. It is about remembering that resources were never meant to be a leash.

There is another quiet form of sovereignty worth naming here. Deb Dana, who teaches about the nervous system, calls them glimmers — micro-moments when your body briefly registers that it is safe. A warm cup held in both hands. A shaft of sunlight across the floor. A pet settling against your leg. The particular way someone you trust says your name. None of these are dramatic. That's the point.

A dysregulated nervous system doesn't learn safety from being told it's safe. It learns safety from noticing, slowly and repeatedly, that it is safe now. Noticing one glimmer today — one small moment where your system registered safe — is a form of reclaiming your attention from threat-scanning. It is a small act of sovereignty too.

You might notice:

• What was one small choice you made today that was just for you? What did it feel like in your body?

• Did any resistance come up — a voice saying it was selfish, wasteful, too much? Whose voice was it?

• Is there a choice you could make tomorrow, no bigger than a drink or a yes or a no, that would belong only to you?

• What does it feel like to imagine that no one is keeping score of these choices anymore?

• What is one small glimmer you noticed today — a micro-moment of ease, warmth, or safety that your nervous system registered?

Low-cost sovereignty, in practice

A few small shapes autonomy can take. None of these need to be justified, performed, or explained:

  • Choosing a drink based on what you actually want, not what's easiest or expected.
  • Saying no to a small request without explaining, apologizing, or softening.
  • Lingering somewhere — a window, a chair, a walk — a moment longer than feels "productive."
  • Listening to a song, reading a book, or watching a show the system would have disapproved of.
  • Wearing something that feels like you, not like a role.
  • Changing your mind about a small plan, with no performance of having a good reason.
  • Letting yourself be bored for ten minutes without reaching for a task.
  • Saying I don't know yet and meaning it, instead of producing a quick answer.

Part 6: The Bond Wasn't a Flaw

One of the most disorienting parts of leaving is how much you still miss them — the partner, the parent, the sibling, the caregiver, the family, the community, the teacher, the version of home that living in the system created. The tenderness shows up uninvited. A song, a smell, a phrase, a particular quality of evening light, and suddenly you're aching for a person, a family, or a life that you know was not safe for you. It can make you doubt everything: your leaving, your knowing, your sanity.

Missing them is a biological response to intermittent reinforcement. It means the bond worked as designed — not that leaving was wrong. Cycles of warmth and withdrawal, closeness and cruelty, create some of the strongest attachments the human nervous system can form. What felt like loyalty or love was, in many moments, the survival mechanism it had to be. That's not a character flaw. That's a nervous system doing exactly what nervous systems do when they are placed inside a system designed to produce that bond.

You can miss someone and still be right to have left. Both can be true in the same breath.

You might notice, without needing to analyze or resolve anything:

• When missing them shows up, what does it feel like in your body — where does it live, how does it move?

• What specifically are you missing in this moment? (A person, a version of them, a version of yourself, a sense of belonging, a rhythm, a role — anything is allowed.)

• If this missing were allowed to simply be here, without deciding anything about what it means, what would it need — a breath, a walk, a witness, a good cry, nothing at all?

You are allowed to grieve this

Missing them does not mean you should go back. It does not mean you didn't love yourself enough to leave. It does not mean your knowing was wrong. It means your body formed a bond inside a system engineered to produce one — and that bond doesn't dissolve on the same day the relationship does. The grief is real. It deserves room. You do not have to earn the right to feel it.

Part 7: Non-Linear Permission

Healing from coercive control is not a staircase. You will loop. You will have a week of strong, grounded knowing — and then a day that feels like the ground has gone out from under you, and you cannot remember why you left. Those days are not regression. They are often the body processing something it wasn't safe to feel before, surfacing now because you finally have the stability to hold it.

You are allowed to loop. You are allowed to grieve in pieces. You are allowed to not be done.

You may also find yourself grieving the version of you who never got to exist inside that system — the one whose time, choices, attention, and growth belonged to someone else. They are not lost. They are arriving now, late and real. Part of the work is letting them in without rushing to make up for the years.

You might notice:

• Think of the most recent hard day — the one that felt like going backward. Looking at it now, what might your body or heart have actually been processing?

• Were you being unkind to yourself on that day, or were you tender with it? What would tender look like next time?

• What do you still miss about the system, the person, or the life you left? (Missing is not the same as wanting to return.)

• What permission do you most need to give yourself right now — and who would you be if you gave it?

Explicit permissions

You are allowed to:

  • Grieve the years. The ones you spent inside, and the ones that were supposed to come after.
  • Miss people who were not safe for you. Affection and clear sight can exist in the same heart.
  • Feel fury and tenderness in the same hour, about the same person, and have both be true.
  • Not be sure yet — about God, the future, your own feelings, what it all meant.
  • Rest. Rest is not regression. Rest is where integration happens.
  • Take longer than anyone else thinks you should. This is your pace. It is the right pace.

Continue Exploring

These are some of the voices that have been companions to this work. None of them is homework. Take what serves. Leave what doesn't.

Books

  • Coercive Control: How Men Entrap Women in Personal Life by Evan Stark — the text that named what you lived as a liberty crime rather than a private failing. Academic in places, but foundational.
  • Why Does He Do That? Inside the Minds of Angry and Controlling Men by Lundy Bancroft — the most accessible map of the inner logic of controlling partners. Plain-spoken, grounding, not academic.
  • Adult Children of Emotionally Immature Parents by Lindsay C. Gibson — if the coercive system was a parent. Gentle, practical, and specifically attuned to the ways a parent's control reshapes an adult child's inner life long after leaving home.
  • Traumatic Narcissism: Relational Systems of Subjugation by Daniel Shaw — a psychoanalyst who writes across contexts (parent, partner, sibling, caregiver, guru, cult leader) about the same underlying dynamic. Denser reading, but among the clearest maps we have of the shared structure beneath all these systems.
  • The Body Keeps the Score by Bessel van der Kolk — the neurobiology of how trauma lives in the body and how healing moves through it. Long and heavy in places; best read when you have some containment around it.
  • The Betrayal Bond: Breaking Free of Exploitive Relationships by Patrick Carnes — on the specific biology of bonds forged through cycles of fear and tenderness, and what unhooks them over time.
  • Freedom of Mind: Helping Loved Ones Leave Controlling People, Cults and Beliefs by Steven Hassan — built around the BITE framework you'll recognize from Part 2. Written for families, but often most useful for the person who has already left.
